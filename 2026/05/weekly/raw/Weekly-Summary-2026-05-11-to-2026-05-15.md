# Part B: Article Scaffold (Public-Safe Synthesis)

## B.1 Central Themes

**Theme 1: Platform Thinking Versus Project Thinking in Healthcare IT**

The week surfaced repeated tension between traditional project management structures and the reality of foundational platform work. A senior stakeholder articulated this directly: "This is a program, not a project." The data platform initiative has no defined end date because it enables capabilities rather than delivering discrete features. This creates friction with governance frameworks designed around bounded deliverables and milestone-based funding.

The practical implication: capability maturity models require different success metrics than feature completion percentages. Assessment moves from "is it done?" to "can we do X now that we could not do before?"

**Theme 2: Multi-Tenancy as Architecture Driver**

Three separate initiatives this week grappled with multi-tenant access patterns across organizational boundaries. The data platform must serve healthcare operations, academic research, and affiliated institutions. The research platform must authenticate students, staff, and external collaborators. The clinical AI spinoff must parameterize deployments for multiple customers.

The common architectural response: identity federation as a first-class concern, not an afterthought. Enclave models for data isolation. Configuration externalization for site-specific variation.

**Theme 3: Agentic Architecture for Operational Scale**

The referral automation project represents a shift from point-to-point integrations (one automation per service line) toward an agentic pattern (one automation that dynamically selects prompts, criteria, and tools based on context). With 470,000 referrals annually across 15 service lines, the economics favor abstraction over replication.

This mirrors broader industry movement toward composable AI systems where orchestration logic lives separately from capability modules.

## B.2 Observations and Patterns

**Observation: Token Economics Shape Architecture**

The clinical AI platform discussion revealed that token-per-minute (TPM) limits constrain automation scaling more than compute capacity. At 800 API calls per patient for FHIR orchestration, the 100,000 automation target requires careful rate management. Architecture must now account for LLM provider quotas as a primary constraint.

**Observation: Legacy Vendor Exit as Business Case Foundation**

Multiple conversations referenced cost savings from replacing incumbent platforms as the primary financial justification for new initiatives. The data platform business case rests on Health Catalyst displacement. The implication: greenfield innovation rarely justifies itself on features alone; it must claim territory currently occupied by paid solutions.

**Observation: Data Quality Remains Qualitative**

When asked for quantitative data quality benchmarks, the response was: "Not quantified." Trust in data remains perception-based. The governance approach acknowledges this: "Trust is more of a qualitative rating... it comes into how we're actually showing trust, so what's our transparency along the way."

This suggests observability and lineage documentation may matter more than precision metrics for adoption.

## B.3 Implications for Practice

**For Solutions Architects:**
- Design for program-level scope even when funded as projects
- Treat identity federation as a foundational requirement, not an enhancement
- Model LLM token consumption as a capacity planning variable

**For Data Platform Teams:**
- Anticipate multi-tenant access patterns from inception
- Document enclave lifecycle including deprovisioning
- Build landing zone patterns for both push and pull ingestion

**For AI/ML Teams:**
- Consider agentic patterns for high-volume, multi-context automations
- Factor TPM constraints into throughput estimates
- Track ROI at the automation level, not just the portfolio level
